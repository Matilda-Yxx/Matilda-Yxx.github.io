Never Been Ready

Posted on 2022-07-23 Views: Valine:

This is a response to the book Selfish, Shallow and Self-absorbed.

I grew up assuming that I’d have my kids. Well, it seemed that people around me would despise every woman who’s past 30 and childless. “The couple must have fertility issues”, people would gossip. Such sentiments were not uncommon in China even until this day. But as I grew up and became more independent, I started to weigh my choices and realized that kids are optional. Ever since that day of revelation, I have been fighting against this “instinct” of becoming a mother. Since I was still undetermined whether I shall have kids, I’ve experienced terrible fears in my most stable and loving relationship - with a partner who is not that into children, who sees them as more of a burden than a blessing. Because of that, I have wept in privacy (which is rather silly an action when I look back); “what will become of us if I want to have babies someday, but not him?” In the beginning, I would revisit this topic with my partner. He’d back in, saying he’s not totally against having kids. He just doesn’t see it happening in a few years. I would then calm down, admitting that it’s not part of my current plan either, considering that I’m still a student with no stable source of income or the right mindset to raise a kid.

No, no, no. I was never ready. I was just not brave enough to say: I will never want to have a baby so let’s cross this thing out from our lifetime to-do list as a couple.

But as more and more of my female friends, including those who declared to me that they love kids and will want to have their own a few years back, started to tell me firmly that they’re not planning to have kids, I feel more relieved - if there are more of us, I would feel better. And most of us are not pathetic, selfish young people lacking maternal love (as some would portray the childless females); instead, we’re caring, independent, and brave women. Most of us love kids; so am I. I used to teach Chinese to kids from 4 to 12 years old; I had a great time with them (mostly), and I was often heartbroken when the tutorship eventually ended. Kids are full of beautiful random imaginations, and they’re such cute little balls of infinite energy. I love them, or at least I enjoyed being their teachers. But when it comes to motherhood, it’s a different story.

Recently I received a few family documents from my parents for some private matter, among which I found my birth certificate. It still strikes me as a surprise that my mom had me when she was 26. I’m 25 this year, I pondered. What would I have known by next year? I certainly don’t feel ready for the grueling responsibilities of motherhood. And my mom told me, now looking back, that she wasn’t prepared at 26, either. She just followed suit since many of her friends already had their babies. Childrearing turned out a huge mess, but she insisted that it was still a big blessing for her. For the first couple of years of my life, she was a full-time mom, so being with me was her career, and she’s proud of how I’ve turned out. But for myself, I probably wished to make achievements in a different type of career. Taking care of a child is way too risky to be considered a career for me. I could be easily disheartened by the capriciousness and unpredictability of the child. I simply could not swallow the possibility that I would have to construct my sole sense of achievement upon something so unreliable.

At the age of 25, having children feels irrelevant. I’m not sure if this will change someday. Before that day, I’ll just keep polishing myself and learn to carry the responsibilities into mid-age. Adulthood is not an easy ride, but I’ve enjoyed making my own choices.
